Issue 01 · Week of April 14, 2026

The fracturing of
peace guarantees

Three continents, one pattern: the simultaneous erosion of ceasefire frameworks is testing diplomatic bandwidth and raising systemic risk for businesses and investors with cross-border exposure.

The briefing

This week, ceasefire frameworks in two active conflict zones came under renewed strain, while a third theater saw its first significant escalation in months. The common thread is not ideology — it is the erosion of deterrence.

International mechanisms that once held frontlines in place are being tested simultaneously, and the diplomatic bandwidth to manage all three is running thin. For executives with supply chain or investment exposure in these regions, the window for orderly risk assessment is narrowing.

Deep dive
Why the Sahel is becoming ungovernable — and what it means for European energy security

The string of coups across West Africa since 2020 is not a coincidence of weak institutions — it is the outcome of a specific security vacuum left by the collapse of the French Barkhane mission and the failure of ECOWAS to project meaningful force. The juntas in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger have turned to Wagner/Africa Corps not out of ideological alignment, but because they offer a transaction: security for mining rights.

What has gone underreported is the downstream energy exposure. Two major European natural gas pipeline projects contingent on Sahel stability — one transiting Niger — are now effectively frozen. The IMF estimates a 14% decline in foreign direct investment into the region in 2025. For European capitals still reconfiguring their energy mix post-Ukraine, the Sahel is no longer a peripheral concern.

The key question for the next six months: whether the AES bloc consolidates into a durable governance model or fractures further under internal militia pressure. The latter scenario carries significantly higher tail risk for regional commodity markets.
